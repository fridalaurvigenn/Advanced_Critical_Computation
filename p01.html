Unveiling the Abyss



The “deep sea” is characterised by extreme conditions that make exploration challenging and fascinating.
Starting at approximately 200 metres (656 feet) and descending to depths surpassing 10,000 metres (32,808 feet),
the deep sea is divided into distinct zones based on depth, such as the mesopelagic,
bathypelagic, abyssopelagic, and hadalpelagic zones.






























One of the defining features of the deep sea is the increasing pressure with depth, reaching extremes that
surpass 1,000 atmospheres or approximately 15,000 pounds per square inch (psi) in places like the Challenger Deep
in the Mariana Trench. The temperature in the deep sea is generally cold and stable, ranging from just abovebr
freezing to a few degrees Celsius, devoid of the temperature fluctuations observed in surface waters.




























Moreover, perpetual darkness shrouds the deep sea beyond a certain depth, as
sunlight is unable to penetrate, prompting the evolution of unique adaptations in the organisms that
call this lightless realm their home. These distinctive characteristics contribute to the allure and scientific
intrigue of the deep sea, where exploration continues to unveil the secrets hidden in its depths.
