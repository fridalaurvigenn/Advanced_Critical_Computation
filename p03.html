Exploring the Depths: Technology


Discuss the tools and technology used for deep-sea exploration. The oceans compose over 71 percent
of the Earth's surface and are the planet's largest ecosystem. Not only that it holds about 99 percent of all habitable
space, with a large variety of organisms thriving from the surface to the scorching waters of our deepest ocean vents
miles below. Submersibles, not very surprisingly have played a key role in exploring and understanding this last frontier.
Reaching astounding depths and capable of performing complicated scientific experiments, these advanced crafts
have even begun to tease out clues to the origin of life the formation of our planet. In this timeline, see just how
imaginatively (some of) these underwater machines have evolved over the decades.



Famous submersibles, ROVs, and AUVs and their roles in uncovering deep-sea mysteries


Trieste (1953) is a Swiss-designed, Italian-built deep-diving
research bathyscaphe(“deep-boat”), made by the Swiss inventor
August Piccard, who in the 1930s reached unprecedented altitudes
of almost 16,000 metres (52,000 feet) with his unique pressurised
aluminium gondola. Piccard modified his atmospheric balloon
concepts to design the Trieste, using gasoline, which is lighter
than water, for buoyancy. In 1960, the submersible dove more than
10,000 metres (35,000 feet) to the deepest point on Earth,
the Marianas Trench, withstanding a crushing pressure
of eight tons per square inch.














































As the oldest manned research submersible, which is
still in operation, Alvin (1964) boasts an impressive résumé.
Having done over 4,000 dives so far, this titanium sphere was
the first manned vessel to visit the wreckage of the Titanic.
The vessel has also helped researchers discover approximately
300 new animal species, including foot-long clams and giant
red-tipped tube worms. Thought to be lost in 1968 when the
support cables failed and it's crew abandoned ship, the craft spent
11 months on the seafloor, sustaining only minor damage.
Near-freezing temperatures and a lack of oxygen kept even
the lunches left onboard perfectly preserved.






Ocean scientists safely travelled to deep-sea coral
ecosystems up to 3000 metres (9,843 feet) below the
ocean's surface inside the Johnson-Sea-Link (1971). It is a
highly manoeuvrable vessel that can accommodate up to
four people, but its chief claim to fame is its use of highly
specialised equipment. Using manipulator arms, suction
devices, and rotating sample-collection tools, the Sea-Link
makes it possible for crew members to execute,
from within the submersible, nearly every
task once performed by divers.
















The first underwater vehicle of her kind, the ABE (1990s)
is a true robot, operating autonomously with no onboard crew
or tethers to guide her. While the small, seven-foot-long vessel
moves at a maximum rate of just two knots, she can cover large
areas of underwater terrain as deep as 5000 metres (16,500 feet) for
months at a time, a task that would prove prohibitively expensive if
attempted with manned or tethered machines. The ABE is
programmed to perform several tasks on her own, such as
navigating, taking photographs, collecting data and
samples, even powering down or "sleeping."



















Scientists designed Hercules (2003), a remotely
operated craft, specifically to study and recover artefacts
from ancient shipwrecks, but it can be used for a wide array of
scientific purposes. Armed with a flexible set of multipurpose tools,
one of the craft's two manipulator arms offers "force feedback,"
allowing operators miles away to better control the pressure the
device applies upon more delicate specimens, such as boxes
found aboard the Titanic . Equipped with a flotation device
composed of glass and epoxy resin, Hercules can "fly" in any
direction like a helicopter and will float gently to the surface if
her thrusters stop turning, an innovation that could
prove useful for future submersibles.























Orpheus (2018) is the new class of autonomous underwater
vehicles (AUV) capable of reaching the ocean’s greatest depths
and the first vehicle of the class. Two identical Orpheus AUVs were
built in 2018, named Orpheus and Eurydice after the famous pair from
Greek mythology who adventured through the depths of Hades.
Access to depths below 6000 metres (around 19,600 feet) has been
historically very limited and fraught with difficulty and risk due to
the immense pressures of extreme depths.
